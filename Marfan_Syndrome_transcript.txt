TRANSCRIPT: Marfan Syndrome
============================================================

So this is what it feels like to live on Mars. These Mars Martians will be our hook for Marfan Syndrome. They have some weird fiber outfits. This fiber outfit represents that Marfan Syndrome is due to a mutation in the FBN1 gene, which encodes Fibrillin 1. This is an autosomal dominant condition shown by the dominant domino the Martian is holding. These Martians have long slim bodies representing that affected patients have a Marfanoid habitus with a tall frame and long extremities. The purple stripes on the Martians symbolize that patients may also lack subcutaneous fat and have striate on the upper chest, arms, thighs and abdomen. Those with Marfan Syndrome also have an increased risk of elastosis per foreign serpigenosa, which typically presents as annular or serpigenous plaques with carototic papules along the rim. This most commonly affects the lateral neck. To represent this, check out this annular crater with carototic mounds along the rim representing the clinical appearance. As a reminder of the location most commonly being on the lateral neck, the Martian is holding its neck. Skeletal manifestations can include a racneodactually where the fingers are long, slender and curved, mimicking the legs of a spider. That is why this Martian is holding a spider in its hand. Even the Martians enjoy looking up at the stars. This Martian, looking upwards with a telescope lens, represents Ectopia lentice, which is an upward displacement of the lens of the eyes seen in about 60% of patients. Cardiovascular manifestations will be shown around this tree that looks like an aorta to represent dilatation of the ascending aorta. This can lead to regurgitation as shown by the Martian regurgitating his food. It can also lead to congestive heart failure, shown by this deflated heart balloon that looks like it is clearly failing. It can also result in dissections, aneurysms and rupture, hence the large dissected hole in the aortic tree. Effective patients can also have mitral valve prolapse, which is shown by this Martian wearing a miter hat. Silly Martians, balloons can survive in space. This popped balloon represents a higher risk of spontaneous pneumothorax in Marfan syndrome patients. In summary, the Martians from Mars serve as the hook to remember Marfan syndrome, starting with their fiber outfits that represent the Fibrillin I gene mutation. The dominant domino shows the conditions autosomal dominant inheritance. Their long slim bodies illustrate the Marfanoid habitus while the purple stripes symbolize striay in areas like the chest, arms, thighs and abdomen due to a lack of subcutaneous fat. The Martian holding its neck highlights the risk of a lasstosis perference serpigenosa, often found on the lateral neck, and the annular crater symbolizes the classic appearance of the plaques. A spider in the Martian's hand reminds us of arachnodactyl and the upward facing stargazing telescope lens symbolizes ectopialentis. Cardiovascular complications are shown by the tree resembling an ascending aorta, which can dilate leading to regurgitation represented by the Martian regurgitating his meal. The deflated heart balloon reflects congestive heart failure while the dissected hole in the aortic tree symbolizes risks of dissection and aneurysms. The Martian with a miter hat signifies mitral valve prolapse, and finally, the pop balloon floating in space shows the risk of spontaneous pneumothorax. Marfan syndrome is really out of this world.
